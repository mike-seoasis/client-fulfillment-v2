How to Choose the Best Running Shoes

Picking the right running shoes starts with understanding your gait, the surfaces you run on, and what your feet need after mile ten. A shoe that works on a treadmill may feel completely different on a gravel trail, so it helps to narrow down where you'll spend most of your time.

In today's competitive running shoe market, brands are pushing boundaries with new foam technology and carbon plate construction. At Acme Running Co., we test every shoe we carry so you don't have to guess about quality.

Cushioning and Support for Long Runs

Long-distance runners need cushioning that holds up past the halfway mark. Look for midsole materials like nitrogen-infused foam or dual-density setups that balance softness with energy return. Our top picks for half-marathon and beyond include shoes from ASICS, Brooks, and New Balance that earned high marks for sustained comfort.

Lightweight Options for Speed Work

When you're running intervals or racing a 5K, every gram counts. Lightweight running shoes strip away excess material while keeping enough stability to protect your joints. Look for shoes under 8 ounces with responsive foam technology and a lower heel drop.

Trail Running vs. Road Running Shoes

The outsole makes all the difference. Trail running shoes use aggressive lug patterns and reinforced toe caps to handle rocks and roots. Road shoes prioritize smooth transitions and breathability. Some runners keep a pair of each — that way the right shoe is always ready by the door.

Finding Your Pronation Type

Your pronation pattern — whether your foot rolls inward, outward, or stays neutral — determines which stability features you need. Neutral runners have the widest selection, while overpronation runners benefit from structured support around the arch. Visit any running store for a free gait analysis, or check the wear pattern on your old shoes for a quick self-assessment.

Our Recommendation Process

Every shoe on this page passed our internal testing: fit check, durability review, and real-mile feedback from our staff runners. We don't just sell shoes; we run in them. That means we can give specific guidance on sizing quirks, break-in periods, and which models run narrow or wide.

When to Replace Your Running Shoes

Most running shoes last 300 to 500 miles, depending on your weight, surface, and running style. If the midsole feels flat or you're noticing new aches in your knees or hips, it's time for a fresh pair. We track your purchase history so we can remind you when a replacement is due.
