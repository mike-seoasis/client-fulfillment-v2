# SKILL BIBLE: Building Brand Guidelines for AI Writing

> **Purpose**: Master framework for creating brand voice documents that feed AI systems consistent, authentic brand context before generating any copy
> **Use Cases**: New client onboarding, brand voice extraction, AI prompt engineering, copywriting briefs, content team alignment
> **Sources**: Nielsen Norman Group, Semrush, HubSpot, Mailchimp Voice & Tone Guide, professional brand strategists
> **Generated**: 2026-01-28

---

## Executive Summary

AI writing tools are only as good as the brand context they receive. Feed them generic prompts, get generic copy. Feed them comprehensive brand guidelines, get copy that sounds like the brand.

This skill bible teaches you how to build brand guideline documents that give AI everything it needs to write authentically on-brand content every time.

**The Brand Context Equation:**
```
Authentic AI Output = (Brand Foundation + Voice Rules + Audience Intel + Examples) Ã— Specificity
```

The more specific your brand guidelines, the less editing you'll do on AI output.

## Part 1: Brand Foundation

### What This Section Captures
The non-negotiable facts about who the brand is, what they do, and why they exist. This is the anchor that keeps all content grounded.

### Brand Foundation Template

```markdown
## Brand Foundation

### Company Overview
- **Company Name**: [Legal name and any DBAs]
- **Founded**: [Year]
- **Location**: [HQ and any other relevant locations]
- **Industry**: [Primary industry/category]
- **Business Model**: [B2B, B2C, DTC, Marketplace, etc.]

### What They Sell
- **Primary Products/Services**: [Main offerings]
- **Secondary Offerings**: [Supporting products/services]
- **Price Point**: [Budget / Mid-range / Premium / Luxury]
- **Sales Channels**: [Online, retail, wholesale, etc.]

### Brand Positioning
- **Tagline/Slogan**: [If they have one]
- **One-Sentence Description**: [How they describe themselves]
- **Category Position**: [Leader, challenger, specialist, disruptor]

### Mission & Values
- **Mission Statement**: [Why they exist]
- **Core Values**: [3-5 guiding principles]
- **Brand Promise**: [What customers can always expect]

### Differentiators
- **Primary USP**: [The #1 thing that makes them different]
- **Supporting Differentiators**: [2-3 additional unique factors]
- **What They're NOT**: [Important distinctions/positioning they reject]
```

### Example: Completed Brand Foundation

```markdown
## Brand Foundation

### Company Overview
- **Company Name**: Eikon Device
- **Founded**: 2019
- **Location**: Los Angeles, CA
- **Industry**: Tattoo Equipment & Supplies
- **Business Model**: DTC E-commerce + Wholesale

### What They Sell
- **Primary Products**: Professional tattoo machines (pens and rotaries)
- **Secondary Offerings**: Needles, cartridges, power supplies, accessories
- **Price Point**: Mid-range to Premium
- **Sales Channels**: Direct website, authorized distributors

### Brand Positioning
- **Tagline**: "Precision Engineered for Artists"
- **One-Sentence Description**: Professional-grade tattoo equipment designed by artists, for artists
- **Category Position**: Premium specialist competing against industry giants

### Mission & Values
- **Mission Statement**: To give tattoo artists reliable tools that let their talent shine
- **Core Values**: Craftsmanship, Artist-First Design, No Compromises on Quality
- **Brand Promise**: Equipment that performs consistently, every session

### Differentiators
- **Primary USP**: Designed with input from working tattoo artists
- **Supporting Differentiators**: Direct customer service, no middleman markup, 2-year warranty
- **What They're NOT**: A mass-market brand, a cheap alternative, a brand that prioritizes marketing over product
```

### Key Questions to Ask Clients

1. "How do you describe your company in one sentence to someone who's never heard of you?"
2. "What's the one thing you do better than anyone else?"
3. "What are you definitely NOT?"
4. "What do customers say when they recommend you to friends?"
5. "If your brand was a person at a party, how would they introduce themselves?"

### Voice Characteristics List

Beyond dimensions, define 3-5 voice characteristics with examples:

```markdown
## Voice Characteristics

### We Are:
1. **Knowledgeable**: We know the craft inside and out. We speak with authority earned from experience.
   - DO: "The 3.5mm stroke length gives you versatility across lining and shading"
   - DON'T: "This product is really good for lots of different things!"

2. **Straightforward**: We say what we mean. No fluff, no hype.
   - DO: "Ships in 2-3 days. Free over $150."
   - DON'T: "Experience the joy of fast shipping on your exciting purchase!"

3. **Supportive**: We're here to help artists succeed, not just sell products.
   - DO: "Questions about needle depth? Our team has your back."
   - DON'T: "Contact customer service for any inquiries."

### We Are NOT:
1. **Hype-driven**: We don't use "revolutionary" or "game-changing"
2. **Condescending**: We never explain things artists already know
3. **Impersonal**: We don't sound like a corporate press release
```

### Example: Completed Style Rules

```markdown
## Writing Style Rules

### Sentence Structure
- **Average sentence length**: 12-18 words (vary for rhythm)
- **Paragraph length**: 2-4 sentences max
- **Use contractions**: Yes, always (we're, you'll, don't)
- **Active vs. passive voice**: Active 90% of time. Passive OK for emphasis ("This machine was built for precision")

### Capitalization
- **Headlines**: Title Case for H1 and H2, Sentence case for H3+
- **Product names**: Always capitalize (EK-3, Falcon Pen)
- **Feature names**: Capitalize if trademarked, lowercase if generic

### Punctuation
- **Serial comma**: Yes, always (needles, cartridges, and grips)
- **Em dashes**: Avoid. Use periods or commas instead.
- **Exclamation points**: Max 1 per page. Earn it.
- **Ellipses**: Avoid. They feel uncertain.

### Numbers
- **Spell out**: One through nine. Numerals for 10+.
- **Currency**: $49 (no decimal for whole dollars)
- **Percentages**: 50% (not "50 percent")

### Formatting
- **Bold**: Key benefits and important terms only. Not for emphasis on everything.
- **Italics**: Product names in running text, technical terms on first use
- **Bullet points**: For lists of 3+. Max 7 items. 1-2 lines each.
- **Headers**: Max 7 words, benefit-driven when possible
```

### Content-Specific Rules

Different content types may need different rules:

```markdown
## Content-Specific Style Rules

### Product Descriptions
- Lead with benefit, not feature
- 100-200 words ideal
- Include 3-5 specs in bullet format
- End with use case or customer scenario

### Collection Pages
- 300-450 words for bottom description
- H2 should include category keyword naturally
- 3-6 internal links
- Top description: 1-2 sentences max

### Email Subject Lines
- 40 characters max
- No ALL CAPS
- Personalization when data available
- No clickbait (deliver on the promise)

### Social Media
- Platform-specific length
- Hashtags: 3-5 max on Instagram, 1-2 on LinkedIn, none on Facebook
- Emojis: Sparingly, brand-appropriate only
```

| FK Irons | Artist collaborations, style-forward | We focus on performance over aesthetics |
| Dragonhawk | Budget-friendly, high volume | We're mid-range with better build quality |
| Bishop | American-made, traditional rotary | We're more versatile across styles |

### Competitive Advantages
- Price-to-quality ratio: Premium performance without luxury markup
- Artist-designed: Real artist input, not just endorsements
- Customer service: Real people who know the equipment
- Warranty: 2 years vs. industry standard 1 year

### Competitive Weaknesses (Internal Only)
- Brand recognition: Not as established as Cheyenne or FK Irons
- Retail presence: Primarily online, limited physical retail
- Product range: Narrower than some competitors

### Positioning Statements

**When comparing to premium brands:**
"Professional performance without the markup."

**When comparing to budget brands:**
"Built to last, not just to sell."

**General differentiation:**
"A lot of machines claim precision. We let the line work speak for itself."

**DO NOT:**
- Name competitors directly in customer-facing copy
- Disparage competitor quality or users
- Make claims we can't substantiate

## Part 10: Building Guidelines from Existing Content

### When Starting from Scratch vs. Extracting from Existing

If the brand already has content, you can reverse-engineer their voice from existing materials.

### Voice Extraction Process

```markdown
## Voice Extraction Worksheet

### Step 1: Gather Samples
Collect 10-20 pieces of existing content:
- Website copy (home page, about page, product pages)
- Email campaigns
- Social media posts
- Customer communications
- Founder interviews or podcasts

### Step 2: Analyze Formality
Read samples aloud. Note:
- Do they use contractions?
- Is language conversational or formal?
- Are sentences short and punchy or long and complex?

Formality rating: ___/10

### Step 3: Analyze Humor
Look for:
- Jokes or puns
- Playful language
- Serious tone throughout

Humor rating: ___/10

### Step 4: Analyze Reverence
Look for:
- How they talk about competitors
- How they talk about the industry
- How they talk about customers

Reverence rating: ___/10

### Step 5: Analyze Enthusiasm
Look for:
- Exclamation points
- Superlatives
- Energy level

Enthusiasm rating: ___/10

### Step 6: Identify Patterns
Note recurring:
- Phrases they repeat
- Words they favor
- Sentence structures they use
- Things they never say

### Step 7: Identify Inconsistencies
Where does the voice feel inconsistent?
These are areas to clarify in the guidelines.
```

### Interview Questions for Voice Discovery

When talking to founders, marketing leads, or stakeholders:

1. "If your brand was a person, who would they be? A celebrity, a character, a type of person?"
2. "What's a brand (in any industry) whose voice you admire?"
3. "What's a brand whose voice you definitely don't want to sound like?"
4. "When customers describe you to friends, what words do they use?"
5. "What's something about your brand that surprises people?"
6. "What's a common misconception about your brand or industry?"
7. "Read this sentence aloud - does it sound like something you'd say?" (test samples)

### Red Flags in Existing Content

When extracting voice from existing content, watch for:

- **Inconsistency**: Different pages sound like different brands
- **Generic language**: Could apply to any competitor
- **AI tells**: Signs previous content was AI-generated without guidelines
- **Outdated positioning**: Company has evolved but content hasn't

These indicate areas where guidelines need to establish clarity, not just document existing practice.
